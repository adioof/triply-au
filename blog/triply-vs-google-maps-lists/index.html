Triply vs Google Maps Saved Places: The Better Travel Bucket List

Apr 11, 2026 · 7 min read

Google Maps has a "Saved" tab. You can create lists. You can star places. You can even share those lists with friends. On the surface, it sounds like everything you need for a travel bucket list. And for years, that's what I used.

But here's the thing I eventually realized: Google Maps Saved Places is a feature that was bolted onto a navigation app. It works. It's functional. But it was never designed with the "I saw this on Instagram and I need to save it" workflow in mind. And that workflow is how most of us actually build our travel wishlists in 2026.

This is a different comparison from Triply vs Google Maps for navigation. Here, I'm specifically looking at the Saved Places and Lists features -- the part of Google Maps people try to use as a bucket list tool -- and how it compares to what Triply is built to do.

Google Maps Lists has the advantage of being part of an app you already use. But when you look at the actual list management and capture experience, the gaps become clear. Let me walk through them.

Where Google Maps Lists Works Fine

It's Already on Your Phone

The best tool is the one you already have. Google Maps is on basically everyone's phone. The Saved tab is right there. You don't need to install anything new, create an account, or learn a new app. You can save a place in two taps while you're already using Maps for something else. That's a real advantage, and I won't pretend it isn't.

Rich Place Information

When you save a place in Google Maps, you get everything Google knows about it: hours, phone number, photos, reviews, price level, how busy it is, website. That's decades of data collection, and it's genuinely useful when you're deciding whether to visit a place. Triply gives you the place name and location, but the depth of business information doesn't compare.

One-Tap Navigation

Saved a restaurant in your Google Maps list? One tap and you've got directions. That seamless flow from "saved place" to "navigating there" is incredibly convenient when you're actually on a trip. Triply doesn't do navigation -- you'd open the place in Google Maps for that anyway.

Shareable Lists

Google Maps lets you share lists with anyone via a link. Planning a trip with friends? Share your "Tokyo Eats" list and everyone can see it, add to it, and navigate to places from it. It's simple and it works. Triply doesn't have sharing yet.

Where Google Maps Lists Falls Short

The Instagram-to-Maps Pipeline Doesn't Exist

This is the big one. You watch a reel about "12 Hidden Gems in Bali." You want all twelve places in your Google Maps. Here's what that process looks like:

  1. Watch the reel. Try to catch the first place name.
  2. Switch to Google Maps.
  3. Search for the place. Hope you spelled the Balinese name correctly.
  4. Find the right one among three similar results.
  5. Save it to a list.
  6. Switch back to Instagram.
  7. Resume the reel. Try to catch place number two.
  8. Repeat ten more times.

Twelve places, twelve app switches, twelve searches, maybe twenty minutes of work. For one reel. And you watch how many travel reels a week? Be honest.

With Triply, you share the reel once. All twelve places appear on a map. Save them to your Bali collection. Maybe fifteen seconds total. That's not a marginal improvement. That's an entirely different experience.

No Source Tracking

After a few months of saving places to Google Maps lists, you'll have dozens of pins and no idea where any of them came from. Why did you save that particular cafe? Was it from a reel? A friend's recommendation? A travel blog? Google Maps doesn't track provenance.

This matters more than you think. When you're planning a trip and looking at your saved list, context is everything. "This place was in that reel where the creator was raving about the handmade pasta" is useful information. "This is a pin you saved at some point for some reason" is not.

Triply links every place back to the original reel. You can trace any recommendation to its source. Months later, you can rewatch the reel and remember exactly why you wanted to go there.

Lists Get Messy Fast

Google Maps lists are flat. You get a name, an optional description, and a list of places. That's it. No categories within lists. No tags. No way to distinguish between "definitely going" and "maybe someday." No separation between restaurants and activities and hotels.

When you're saving three or four places, this is fine. When you've been building a bucket list for six months and you have 200 pins across fifteen lists, it becomes a wall of pins with no hierarchy or organization. You end up with a list called "Want to Go" that has places from thirty different countries, and finding the ones for your upcoming Tokyo trip means scrolling through everything.

Triply's collections are designed around how people actually think about travel. By destination, by trip, by vibe. And the map view means you can always see the geographic spread of your saves, not just a text list.

One Place at a Time, Every Time

Google Maps forces you to add places individually. Search, find, save, repeat. There's no batch import. No "add all places from this content." No way to speed up the process even slightly.

This is fine for the "I'm already here and want to save this restaurant" use case. It's terrible for the "I just watched a reel with ten places and want to save all of them" use case. And the second use case is increasingly how people build travel bucket lists.

Designed for Search, Not Discovery

Google Maps Saved is built around the assumption that you know what you're looking for. You search for a specific place, you find it, you save it. The entire flow starts with a search query.

But travel discovery in 2026 doesn't start with a search query. It starts with a reel you weren't looking for, from a creator you stumbled across, showing places you'd never heard of. The places don't come from your intention -- they come from content you're already consuming. Google Maps has no way to participate in that flow.

The Collection Philosophy

There's a deeper difference worth calling out. Google Maps treats saved places as data points -- coordinates with labels. Triply treats them as discoveries -- places that came from specific content, organized into collections with context and narrative.

A Google Maps list says: "Restaurant Name, Address, 4.5 stars."

A Triply collection says: "Restaurant Name -- from that reel about hidden pasta places in Rome, part of your Italy 2027 dream trip."

Both give you the same location. But one gives you the story, and the story is often what makes you actually visit.

My honest take: Google Maps Saved Places is good enough if you're saving a handful of places you already know about. But as a bucket list tool -- something you build over months of discovering places through social media -- it's frustratingly manual and lacking in organization. Triply was built specifically for that long-term bucket list use case, and it shows.

When Google Maps Lists Work Fine

Stick with Google Maps Saved if:

  • You mostly save places you encounter in real life or find through Google searches
  • You want one-tap navigation from your saved places
  • You need business hours, reviews, and detailed place info alongside your saves
  • You share lists with friends and want them to use Maps for navigation
  • You save a small number of places and don't need complex organization
  • You're on Android

When You Need Triply

Triply is the better choice if:

  • You build your bucket list from Instagram Reels and social media content
  • You're tired of the manual search-and-save process for every place in every reel
  • You want to know where each recommendation came from
  • You have hundreds of saved places and need better organization than flat lists
  • You want to bulk-capture all places from a single reel in seconds
  • You're building a long-term travel wishlist, not just saving nearby restaurants

The Bottom Line

Google Maps Saved Places is a feature. It's a good feature, attached to an incredible app. For saving places you already know about and navigating to them later, it works perfectly.

Triply is a purpose-built bucket list tool. It's designed from the ground up for the specific workflow of turning social media travel content into organized, mapped, contextual collections that you'll actually use to plan trips.

If your bucket list comes from Google searches and word-of-mouth, Google Maps is probably sufficient. If your bucket list comes from Instagram Reels -- and let's be real, more and more of it does -- then you need something designed for that flow. You need something that bridges the gap between "I just saw something amazing" and "that place is saved, mapped, and organized."

Google Maps doesn't bridge that gap. It wasn't designed to. Triply was.

Use Google Maps for navigating. Use Triply for dreaming. Your bucket list will thank you.
